# Suomi Sisu - Strategy

## The Problem

Finnish citizens pay billions annually to foreign corporations for essential digital tools - email, cloud storage, VPN, AI assistants. Profits flow to Silicon Valley. Data leaves Finland. And the people who generate all that value own nothing.

As AI accelerates toward singularity-level disruption, this problem gets worse. Jobs will be automated. Industries will be restructured. The value created by AI will concentrate in the hands of whoever owns the platforms. If Finns don't own their digital infrastructure, they'll be tenants in someone else's future.

## The Idea

A Finnish digital cooperative (osuuskunta) where every user is a shareholder. You pay for services you already need. Profits come back to you. Data stays in Finland. The cooperative can't be acquired, can't pivot to ads, and can't sell your data - it's constitutionally bound to serve its members.

## Why Now

AI changes the economics of building software. A small team can now build tools that previously required hundreds of engineers. Open-source models (Llama, Mistral, and others) mean you don't need Google's budget to deliver AI-powered services. The barrier to entry has collapsed - but only for those willing to build.

This means a cooperative can compete on product quality, not just on values. That's new. Five years ago this would have been a nice idea with mediocre tools. Today it's a nice idea with genuinely competitive tools.

## The Cooperative Model

Structure: Finnish osuuskunta under osuuskuntalaki (421/2013)

- One member, one vote
- Small membership fee (e.g. ~EUR 20, refundable on exit)
- Surplus distributed to members proportional to usage (ylijaamanpalautus)
- Can't be acquired by a corporation - members would have to vote to dissolve
- Follows the proven playbook of S-Group (2.4M members) and OP Group (2M members)

The math:

- Every subscriber becomes a cooperative member
- Surplus distribution proportional to how long you've been a member and how much you use
- Long-term members accumulate more weight - rewards loyalty, prevents gaming
- Early adopters benefit from smaller member pool, but value stabilizes as the cooperative grows

The loyalty flywheel:

The longer you're a member and the more you use, the larger your surplus share. Use more, own more. Stay longer, earn more. Your stake in the cooperative grows through real usage, backed by actual infrastructure, real revenue, and services you use every day.

## Phase 1: VPN (Proof of Concept)

Why VPN first:

- Technically simplest (WireGuard, open source, battle-tested)
- Lowest trust requirement - if something breaks, nobody loses their data permanently
- "Your traffic stays in Finland" is an immediately understandable pitch
- Competing against shady providers with murky ownership (NordVPN, etc.)
- A small team can build and launch in weeks
- Proves the cooperative model works before taking on harder challenges

Target: EUR 5/month. First 1,000 members via waitlist.

Success criteria: Reliable service, costs covered, first surplus distribution (even if small). Members see the model works.

## Phase 2: Cloud Storage

- Built on Nextcloud (open source, mature)
- 200GB per user on Finnish servers
- Natural extension of Phase 1 infrastructure
- Start introducing AI features: smart search, file organization, Finnish-language document understanding

Bundle price: EUR 10/month for VPN + Storage.

## The AI & Singularity Angle

We are heading toward a world where AI automates large parts of the economy. When that happens, the question isn't whether value gets created - it will, enormously. The question is: who captures that value?

In the current model, it flows to platform owners - a handful of corporations and their shareholders. Most citizens are left as consumers paying for access to AI they don't own.

Cooperatives offer a different path. If citizens collectively own the AI tools they use, the value created by automation flows back to them - not as charity or UBI, but as surplus from a business they co-own and actively use. This isn't a safety net. It's ownership.

The more AI can do, the more valuable cooperative membership becomes. As tools get smarter and costs drop, the surplus per member grows. A cooperative that starts with a VPN today could be distributing meaningful income to its members in a decade - not because anyone planned a welfare program, but because the members own the machines that do the work.

## Open Source & Trust

Trust is everything. A new cooperative asking people to route their internet traffic, store their files, and host their email needs to earn trust from zero.

The strategy:

- All infrastructure code is open source. Anyone can audit what we run. No black boxes, no "trust us." Verify it yourself.
- Independent security audits published publicly, funded by the cooperative.
- Transparency reports on finances, data requests, infrastructure decisions.
- Member governance - major decisions go to a vote. Members decide the roadmap, the privacy policy, the surplus distribution.

Open source is not just a technical choice. It's a trust architecture. When you can read the code, you don't need to trust the people. And when the people are also the owners, incentives align completely.

This is fundamentally different from a corporation publishing open-source code as a marketing move. The cooperative's members ARE the stakeholders. There is no hidden profit motive. The code is open because the members demanded it, and the members are the owners.
